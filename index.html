The Framework
THE FOUR COLORS

Each color represents a fundamental behavioral style — not a personality type, not a box. Most people lead with one or two colors. Understanding yours is the start. Understanding others' is the superpower.

Dominant · D in DISC
RED
"Just tell me the result."
🔴
▼
Core Traits
Decisive and action-oriented
Confident under pressure
Results-driven and competitive
Takes ownership and initiative
Can be controlling or domineering
Impatient with slow processes
Appears cold or dismissive
Struggles to listen deeply
At Their Best & Worst
When thriving
  • Drives the team through a crisis
  • Makes the hard call no one else will
  • Achieves goals others thought impossible
  • Holds everyone accountable, including themselves
When stressed
  • Becomes controlling and micromanaging
  • Cuts people off mid-sentence
  • Runs over feelings to hit the goal
  • Interprets caution as weakness
Core Fear
Being seen as weak, incompetent, or losing control of an outcome that matters to them. Reds fear being taken advantage of or having their authority undermined.
What Motivates Them
  • Winning — outcomes, rankings, visible results
  • Autonomy and authority to make decisions
  • Challenges that test their limits
  • Direct recognition for their accomplishments
How to Spot a Red
  • Speaks in short, direct sentences — minimal small talk
  • Body language is forward-leaning and assertive
  • Often the first to speak and the last to back down
  • Gets visibly frustrated when meetings go in circles
  • Uses words like "results," "accountability," "bottom line"
Inspiring · I in DISC
YELLOW
"Imagine if we could…"
🟡
▼
Core Traits
Energetic and charismatic
Optimistic and enthusiastic
Creative and idea-generating
Connects with people naturally
Can overpromise and underdeliver
Loses interest in execution
Can dominate conversations
Avoids difficult feedback
At Their Best & Worst
When thriving
  • Lights up a room and raises team energy
  • Sells a vision nobody believed was possible
  • Builds instant rapport with new people
  • Turns a low-morale situation around fast
When stressed
  • Becomes scattered and inconsistent
  • Talks over people or dominates the room
  • Overpromises to keep everyone happy
  • Avoids hard conversations entirely
Core Fear
Being ignored, rejected, or seen as irrelevant. Yellows fear losing their audience or being excluded from the group. Silence from others feels like disapproval.
What Motivates Them
  • Public recognition and genuine praise
  • Creative freedom and new experiences
  • Being liked and included by the group
  • Opportunities to inspire and influence others
How to Spot a Yellow
  • Lots of hand gestures, facial expressions, vocal variety
  • Tells stories instead of making points
  • Remembers everyone's names — and personal details
  • Gets visibly deflated when ignored or talked over
  • Uses words like "amazing," "exciting," "imagine," "we should"
Stable · S in DISC
GREEN
"Is everyone okay with this?"
🟢
▼
Core Traits
Calm, steady, and reliable
Deeply empathetic and loyal
Excellent listener and team anchor
Consistent and trustworthy
Avoids confrontation to a fault
Resistant to sudden change
Holds feelings in too long
Can be passive under pressure
At Their Best & Worst
When thriving
  • Is the glue that holds a fractured team together
  • Builds deep, lasting trust with colleagues
  • Creates psychological safety for the whole group
  • Delivers consistently without needing a spotlight
When stressed
  • Goes silent and withdraws from the group
  • Agrees outwardly while disagreeing internally
  • Lets resentment build rather than voicing it
  • Becomes rigid and resistant to any change
Core Fear
Conflict, instability, and losing the security of trusted relationships. Greens dread being forced into confrontation or having to choose sides among people they care about.
What Motivates Them
  • Harmony and a stable, predictable environment
  • Feeling genuinely valued and appreciated
  • Long-term relationships built on real trust
  • Having time to adjust before major changes hit
How to Spot a Green
  • Asks how others are feeling before stating their own view
  • Rarely the first to raise a concern — waits to be asked
  • Steady, unhurried tone even in tense situations
  • Will say yes when they mean "I need more time to think"
  • Uses words like "together," "everyone," "comfortable," "okay with"
Analytic · C in DISC
BLUE
"Where's the data on this?"
🔵
▼
Core Traits
Precise, methodical, thorough
High personal standards
Catches errors before they happen
Logical and evidence-based
Can get paralyzed by analysis
Overly critical — of self and others
Seen as cold or detached
Slow to act without full information
At Their Best & Worst
When thriving
  • Catches the critical flaw nobody else saw
  • Builds systems that actually work long-term
  • Brings rigor and credibility to any decision
  • Produces work that is genuinely excellent
When stressed
  • Gets lost in details and misses the deadline
  • Becomes hypercritical of everyone's work
  • Withdraws emotionally from the team
  • Refuses to commit without more information
Core Fear
Being wrong, being criticized, or having their work exposed as flawed. Blues fear public failure or being seen as incompetent — which is why they over-prepare.
What Motivates Them
  • High quality — doing things properly matters deeply
  • Having enough time and information to do it right
  • Respect for their expertise and thoroughness
  • Clear expectations and well-defined processes
How to Spot a Blue
  • Asks clarifying questions before committing to anything
  • Takes notes, builds lists, documents everything
  • Pauses before answering — they're processing, not hesitating
  • Reluctant to share opinions until certain of their ground
  • Uses words like "specifically," "verify," "process," "evidence"
How the Colors Collide
WORKING TOGETHER

Understanding your own color is step one. Understanding how it interacts with others is where the real work — and the real payoff — happens.

HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH EACH COLOR
TALKING TO A RED
  • Lead with the bottom line. State your point in the first sentence. They'll stop listening if you take too long to get there.
  • Be direct and confident. Hedging or apologizing reads as weakness. Say what you mean.
  • Give them options, not orders. Reds resist being told what to do. Present choices and let them feel ownership.
  • Keep it brief. Bullet points over paragraphs. Outcomes over process. Results over feelings.
  • Don't take bluntness personally. It's not aggression — it's just their default mode. They move on fast.
  • Challenge them respectfully. They actually respect people who push back with confidence. Agree too easily and they'll stop taking you seriously.
TALKING TO A YELLOW
  • Be warm and engaged. Match their energy. Cold or flat delivery shuts them down immediately.
  • Let them talk. Yellows process out loud. Don't rush them to conclusions — they'll get there.
  • Use stories and examples. Abstract logic lands flat. Vivid, relatable examples land every time.
  • Give genuine recognition. A sincere compliment before hard feedback makes all the difference.
  • Tie things to people. Frame your message around impact on the group or relationships, not just tasks.
  • Put important things in writing. Yellows get excited in the moment but may not retain details. Follow up key commitments via email or text.
TALKING TO A GREEN
  • Give them advance notice. Surprises — even good ones — are stressful for Greens. A heads-up goes a long way.
  • Create psychological safety. They won't share honest opinions in an environment that feels unsafe. Ask directly and privately.
  • Be patient. Greens need time to think before responding. Silence isn't disengagement — it's processing.
  • Acknowledge their contribution. Greens work hard and quietly. They need to know their efforts are seen.
  • Don't push for immediate decisions. They prefer to sit with things. If they're forced to decide on the spot, they'll say yes when they mean no.
  • Ask how they're feeling — and mean it. They can tell when you're being performative. Genuine interest builds enormous trust with Greens.
TALKING TO A BLUE
  • Come prepared. Bring data, documentation, and well-reasoned arguments. Vague claims get dismissed.
  • Give them time to think. Don't pressure them for immediate answers. Their best thinking happens after the meeting.
  • Respect their process. Don't shortcut the steps they've laid out — it breaks their trust fast.
  • Be precise with language. Exaggerations and hyperbole irritate Blues. Say what you actually mean.
  • Acknowledge their quality. Recognize the rigor they bring — not just the outcome, but the care that went into it.
  • Don't take criticism personally. When Blues raise problems, they think they're being helpful. They genuinely are — they just skip the packaging.
COLOR COMBINATIONS: TENSION & STRENGTH

Every color pairing has natural friction points — and natural strengths. Understanding the dynamic helps you work with it instead of against it.

Red + Yellow
Natural alliance. Both are extroverted and move fast. Red provides direction; Yellow provides buy-in. Tension arises when Red steamrolls Yellow's need for recognition.
Red + Green
Classic tension. Red moves fast; Green needs stability. Red reads Green as weak. Green reads Red as reckless. When it works, Red protects Green's space and Green softens Red's edges.
Red + Blue
Productive friction. Red wants to move; Blue wants to be right. Red finds Blue slow. Blue finds Red sloppy. Together: they make bold decisions that actually hold up.
Yellow + Green
Warm and connected. Both are people-focused. Yellow brings energy; Green brings depth. Risk: they prioritize harmony over hard conversations and avoid conflict together.
Yellow + Blue
Opposites in method. Yellow leads with feeling; Blue leads with logic. Yellow annoys Blue with imprecision. Blue frustrates Yellow with coldness. Together: creative ideas that actually work.
Green + Blue
Quiet powerhouse. Both are introverted and careful. Green values harmony; Blue values accuracy. Together they create stable, high-quality work — but may need a Red or Yellow to drive urgency.

OBSERVATION GUIDE — Quick Reference
RED signals
  • Jumps to solutions immediately
  • Interrupts or talks over people
  • Gets impatient in slow meetings
  • Makes eye contact to assert control
  • "Just tell me what needs to happen"
YELLOW signals
  • Tells stories, uses analogies
  • Expressive face and big gestures
  • Knows everyone's names quickly
  • Talks fast, jumps between ideas
  • "This is going to be SO good"
GREEN signals
  • Asks "how does everyone feel?"
  • Slow to share their own opinion
  • Nods a lot, actively listens
  • Checks in on others privately
  • "As long as everyone's okay with it"
BLUE signals
  • Takes detailed notes
  • Asks clarifying questions first
  • Pauses before responding
  • Points out what's missing
  • "Can you send me that in writing?"
